Policy language is necessary, but policy language does not enforce registry exchange by
itself.
This page explains why safeguards need inspectable metadata, configured runtime behavior,
and audit records that reviewers can use after a trust-critical request.

## The problem

Registry integrations often rely on policy documents, memoranda, or access approvals that
sit outside the technical surface.
The API may not know the caller purpose, allowed scope, disclosure limit, or audit
requirement.

When those controls are not enforced by the runtime path, a caller can receive more data
than the approved purpose requires or make requests that are hard to reconstruct later.

## Why it matters

Safeguards are operational properties.
They depend on what the service accepts, what the service returns, what the service logs,
and what reviewers can inspect before and after integration.

This matters for both registry-service readiness and evidence exchange.
A protected read route needs scope checks and audit behavior.
A claim or credential path needs disclosure rules, issuer configuration, and evidence of
the evaluation event.

## Why common approaches stall

Governance-only approaches can state the intended control.
They stall when the API does not carry enough structure to enforce or audit the control.

Application-only controls can work inside one system.
They stall across institutions when the registry surface, workflow tool, exchange layer,
wallet, and reviewer cannot inspect the same contract.

## What Registry Stack changes

Registry Stack moves safeguards into metadata and runtime behavior.
Registry Manifest can publish policy and evidence-offering metadata.
Registry Relay enforces configured scopes and writes audit records for requests.
Registry Witness enforces configured claim rules, disclosure modes, delegated-evaluation
peer policy, and credential issuance paths.
Registry Platform provides shared primitives for authentication, audit envelopes, HTTP
safety, cryptography, OIDC, and SD-JWT handling.

The result is not automated governance.
The result is a technical surface where governance choices can be configured, enforced,
and reviewed.

## What is built today

The current docs describe Relay API-key and OIDC modes, dataset-scoped scopes, projected
fields, declared filters, and tamper-evident audit records.
They describe Witness disclosure modes, configured claim evaluation, delegated evaluation,
credential issuance, OID4VCI routes, and audit-stamped results.
They also describe Manifest policy and evidence-offering render outputs.

See [DPI safeguards alignment](../../explanation/dpi-safeguards-alignment/),
[Authorize callers](../../projects/registry-relay/authorize-callers/), and
[Evidence issuance](../../explanation/evidence-issuance/).

## What is future-facing or out of scope

Registry Stack does not decide legal basis, institutional accountability, or programme
eligibility.
It also does not replace security operations, incident response, retention policy, or
human review.

The future Trust Control Plane is an operations direction, not a current v2 product
surface.
